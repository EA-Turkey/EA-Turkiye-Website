Effective altruism is best approached not as a creed, but as a question: **given limited time, money, attention, and institutions, how can we help others as much as possible?** The “effective” part matters because good intentions are common and resources are scarce; the “altruism” part matters because the point is to use those resources for others’ benefit rather than only for ourselves.

That question changes how many people compare problems. If two ways of helping are both decent, but one helps far more, that seems morally relevant. If one problem affects millions of people or animals while another affects thousands, that matters too. If a problem is large but already crowded with money and talent, that changes the picture. Much of effective-altruist reasoning begins with three simple lenses: **scale**, **neglectedness**, and **tractability**. These are not a magic formula. They are a disciplined way of comparing options that otherwise feel hard to compare.

Another recurring idea is **expected value**: taking seriously both the size of an outcome and the chance that it could happen. This is familiar from public health, insurance, medicine, disaster planning, and policy. In effective altruism, it helps explain why some people focus not only on ongoing suffering, but also on low-probability, very high-stakes risks. That leads into **cause prioritisation**: not just asking what would be good to do, but what might be among the best things to do, given the evidence we have and the person we are.

This style of reasoning has led many people toward a cluster of cause areas. One is **global health and development**, where relatively modest amounts of money can still prevent disease, treat illness, or improve lives in measurable ways. Another is **animal welfare**, especially the treatment of animals in industrial farming, where the scale of suffering is immense and public attention remains limited. A third broad area concerns **global catastrophic risks** such as pandemics, nuclear risks, and some risks from advanced AI systems. Related to this is **global priorities research**, which asks how we should compare problems in the first place. Not everyone who is interested in effective altruism ends up prioritising the same things, but the shared habit is comparative judgement rather than moral drift.

It is also worth saying what effective altruism is not. It is not a requirement to become a utilitarian, to ignore politics, or to dismiss systemic change. People in and around the community work on public policy, institutional reform, research, philanthropy, governance, movement building, and career choice. There is no single effective-altruist lifestyle, and there are serious internal disagreements about methods, priorities, and tone.

Those disagreements matter because the movement has had real controversies. The collapse of FTX and the role of people associated with effective altruism damaged trust and raised hard questions about governance, culture, due diligence, and moral overconfidence. **Longtermism** has also been criticised for relying on speculative arguments, for being too ready to trade present needs against uncertain future gains, and for drifting toward elite, technocratic decision-making. More broadly, critics have argued that effective altruism can underweight justice, democracy, dignity, and the perspectives of people most affected by global problems. Those are not side notes. Anyone taking the ideas seriously should read the criticisms too.

What remains after the disagreement and the controversy is still worth engaging. The strongest part of effective altruism is the insistence that helping others is not only about sincerity, but also about judgement; that evidence should matter; that some problems really are more neglected or more urgent than others; and that individuals can sometimes do much more good than they first imagine. You do not need to agree with every conclusion to find that framework useful.

A good way to use this page is simple. Start with one general introduction and one critique. Then pick **one** cause area rather than five. If you find yourself wanting to do more, move to the careers, giving, or programmes section. The goal is not to absorb a canon. It is to become a little more careful about one of the oldest questions there is: **what should we do, if we want our lives to be genuinely useful to others?**
